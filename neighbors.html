01 · The Basics

What ReDew Anson is — and why it's here.

Quick orientation before the detailed questions.

What are you building?

An energy facility in Jones County, Texas, anchored by 600 MW of long-duration vanadium flow battery storage and interconnected to the ERCOT grid at the existing Phantom Hill 345 kV substation. The storage is the core of the project: it holds electricity when there is plenty of it and releases it back to the grid when Texas needs it most.

Is this a power plant?

No. We do not generate power. We store power that has already been generated — from solar, wind, gas, and other sources feeding the ERCOT grid — and we release it when demand spikes. No combustion. No smokestack. No air emissions from the storage system.

Is this solar? Wind?

No. The site has no solar panels and no wind turbines. ReDew Anson works alongside the generators that already exist on the ERCOT grid, holding their output and dispatching it when it's needed.

Why this specific piece of land?

Two reasons. First, the land. This parcel has been used for dryland farming, but for more than a decade the yields have run at roughly 25% of what they should be — the water table has dropped, the rainfall has shifted, and the economics of cropping it have gone with them. We are not taking productive cropland out of production. We are putting underused land back to work. Second, the grid. The Phantom Hill 345 kV substation sits right next door — one of the most strategically valuable interconnection points in ERCOT West. The right land happens to be next to the right node on the grid. That doesn't happen by accident, and it doesn't happen often.

Is this all you're planning to build on the site?

The 600 MW long-duration storage facility is the core of the project, and it's what we're building first. Energy infrastructure of this scale is designed to support the grid and the customers around it, and we expect the facility to evolve as we work with grid operators and energy partners. Anything that changes the footprint, the look, or the impact on the community will get communicated to the community openly. We're not in the business of surprises.

When does construction start and finish?

We're on track for notice-to-proceed in mid-2027, with construction running roughly 18 months from that point and commercial operations late 2028.

02 · Safety

Safety for your family, your land, and your water.

The single most important reason we chose this technology over the alternatives.

Is there a fire risk?

No. This project uses long-duration vanadium flow chemistry — not lithium-ion. The electrolyte is water-based. It doesn't burn. It cannot enter thermal runaway the way lithium-ion batteries can. The headlines you've seen about battery fires — those were lithium-ion projects. This is a fundamentally different technology, deliberately chosen for that reason.

What's inside the tanks?

Vanadium dissolved in a water-based electrolyte, fully contained in sealed tanks engineered for zero release under normal operations. Vanadium is a stable, recyclable metal used in steel manufacturing for over a century.

Will this affect our drinking water or the aquifer?

The storage facility consumes essentially no water in operation. We do not draw from local wells, the aquifer, or municipal supply to run it. The system is sealed and engineered to prevent any release into soil or groundwater, with secondary containment built into the site design.

Does building here help or hurt the water table?

It helps. This land has been worked as dryland farming for decades, with declining yields tied directly to a falling water table and shifting rainfall. Continuing to farm it means continuing to attempt extraction from a depleted aquifer for diminishing returns. Taking this parcel out of marginal farming — and putting a facility on it that uses essentially no water — is a net positive for the local water table, not a draw on it.

Does the project use water during construction?

Yes, in limited amounts and not from local supply. Construction water for concrete, dust control, and site work is delivered from off-site sources. We do not pull from local wells, neighboring landowners, or municipal supply, and we do not draw the parcel's groundwater. The total construction-period draw is a fraction of what an equivalent footprint of irrigated farming would use in a single season.

What happens to rainwater and runoff on the site?

Site engineering keeps stormwater on the parcel. Grading, retention, and drainage are designed to contain rainfall and release it slowly, not dump runoff onto neighboring property or into local drainages. The civil design is reviewed as part of the permitting process — this is not optional, and it is not a corner anyone gets to cut.

What about water for fire suppression or emergencies?

Vanadium flow batteries do not burn. There is no thermal-runaway fire risk to suppress, which is one of the reasons we chose this technology. A small, sealed emergency-water reserve is standard for any industrial site — it sits in storage tanks on the parcel and is not drawn from local wells or municipal supply.

How does the water use compare to other power projects?

It is not close. Traditional gas-fired power plants use thousands of gallons per day for cooling. Lithium-ion battery projects use significant water for cell cooling and fire suppression. The long-duration vanadium flow storage facility we're building uses essentially none. The water profile is one of the main reasons this technology was chosen for West Texas in the first place.

What about toxic chemicals or emissions?

There is no combustion, no smokestack, no air emissions, and no ongoing waste stream from operations. The electrolyte stays inside the system for the project's life and is fully recyclable at end of life — the vanadium is recovered and reused, not landfilled.

Who shows up if something goes wrong?

The local fire authority and county emergency services are part of the project from the design stage forward. They get trained on the site, they receive the equipment information, and they have a direct line to our operations team. The site is fenced, monitored 24/7, and built to industrial safety standards.

03 · Look & Sound

What it looks like. What it sounds like.

We didn't want a fenced container yard. We wanted something worthy of the land it sits on.

What does it look like?

A purpose-designed energy campus. We commissioned architecture-driven building design specifically so this site would not feel like a typical industrial yard. The buildings are world-class — clean lines, deliberate materials, low profile, designed to complement the West Texas horizon rather than fight it. Landscaped grounds. No towers. No spinning blades. A facility you can drive past and recognize as belonging here.

Will it block our view?

No. The buildings are low-profile and the active footprint sits well back from the road and from property lines. The long horizon lines that make this part of West Texas what it is — those stay open.

Will we hear it?

Battery storage is one of the quieter pieces of grid infrastructure. The main sound sources are cooling equipment and inverters, engineered to stay at or below ambient noise levels at the property line. No turbines. No combustion engines. No rail noise. From down the road, you will not hear it.

What about lights at night?

Security lighting only, directed downward and shielded, designed to stay on the site and out of your sky. We chose West Texas because the land works — not because we want to wash it in floodlights.

How big is the footprint?

The active equipment and building footprint is a fraction of the total parcel. The rest of the land stays open. The site layout deliberately sets the facility well back from neighbors and from the road.

04 · Construction

What construction looks like — and how we keep it from being a headache.

The temporary side of any project. We take this seriously because it's the part you'll feel.

How long will construction take?

Roughly 18 months from notice-to-proceed to commercial operations. The heaviest traffic and equipment deliveries are concentrated in defined windows within that period, not constant across all 18 months.

Will heavy trucks tear up our roads?

We work with Jones County on route planning before construction starts, and we coordinate on road maintenance that results from project-related traffic. We don't want a finished project sitting at the end of a torn-up road any more than the county does.

Construction noise and dust?

Standard daylight construction hours. Standard dust control practices on site access roads and laydown areas. We publish a community point of contact during construction — if something isn't right, we want to hear about it directly, not through a complaint chain.

Will construction workers come from out of state?

We prioritize Texas-based contractors and Texas trades wherever the work supports it. Some specialized crews travel for projects like this regardless of where they're built — that's industry-wide — but the general construction labor leans Texas.

05 · Economics & Jobs

What this puts back into Jones County.

The economic case for the community. Specific dollar figures get firmed up as the project moves through development — the structure is what matters.

What does ReDew Anson bring to Jones County?

A project of this size adds meaningful, long-term property tax revenue to the Jones County tax rolls and to the local Independent School District — revenue that supports schools, roads, emergency services, and county operations for the life of the project. That funding does not come out of any homeowner's pocket. It's new revenue, paid by the project, for the community.

Will it create local jobs?

Yes. Construction employs a meaningful workforce during the build period, with subcontracting opportunities for Texas-based trades — electrical, civil, mechanical, fencing, site prep, fueling, food service, and more. Once operating, the site supports skilled long-term technician and operations roles.

Will you hire locally?

Where the work fits, yes. Texas labor and Texas suppliers are the default — not because it sounds good, but because local crews who live in the community do better work and stay longer.

Will this hurt my property value?

No. The reasons are practical: low visual profile, architecturally designed buildings, minimal noise, no smokestack, no continuous truck traffic after construction, no land subdivision around the site. The land around the project stays usable for the things it has always been used for.

Will my electric bill go down?

We're not going to promise that. What we will say plainly: battery storage on the ERCOT grid helps moderate the price volatility Texans saw in February 2021 and during hot-summer peaks. More storage on the system is a structural benefit for ratepayers over time, even if you can't draw a straight line from one project to one month's bill.

Are you getting tax abatements?

We engage with the county and the school district on the standard tools Texas uses to attract major capital investment. Any abatement structure is negotiated in public, on the record — not in a side deal. The goal is a fair outcome that brings the project to Jones County and makes the community a meaningful long-term beneficiary.

06 · Community Impact

Schools, reliability, and being a good neighbor.

Beyond the tax line items — the way this fits into the community.

What does this do for our schools?

The local ISD is one of the largest direct beneficiaries of property tax revenue from a project like this. That funding can support facilities, teacher retention, programs, and stability over the long life of the project. We see that as one of the most important things this project does, not a side effect of it.

Will this help with grid reliability?

That is the whole point. Texans remember February 2021. They remember summer 2023. Battery storage is one of the most effective tools we have to firm the grid against demand spikes and supply gaps — and Phantom Hill is one of the most strategic interconnection points in ERCOT West for that purpose.

Will you support the community beyond taxes?

Yes. The specifics get worked out in conversation with the community, not in a press release — but the principle is simple: if this project is going to benefit from being built here, the community deserves to benefit from us being here.

What about agriculture and ranching on neighboring land?

The project doesn't affect surrounding agricultural operations. No water draw on shared resources, no chemical drift, no field disruption. Ranching and farming continue as they have.

07 · End of Life

What happens at the end of the project.

A fair question that every neighbor deserves a clear answer to before the project gets built — not after.

How long will it operate?

Design life is 20+ years of active operation, with ongoing maintenance and component refresh over that period. Vanadium flow electrolyte doesn't degrade the way lithium cells do — the active material can be reused effectively indefinitely.

What happens when it's done?

A decommissioning plan is part of the project from the start, including financial assurance to cover removal of the equipment and restoration of the site. The community shouldn't carry that cost; the project does. That commitment is part of the permitting and approval process, not a handshake.

Is this going to leave a mess behind?

No. Vanadium flow systems are designed to be recyclable — most of the active material is recovered and reused rather than landfilled. The site itself is engineered to be restored when the project ends.

08 · Who We Are

Who's actually behind this.

Plain answer: a Texas company, founder-led, accountable in person.

Who owns ReDewable?

ReDewable Energy Company LLC is a Texas-registered, founder-led company. Talor Byington is the founder — a Texas A&M graduate with a background in Texas construction, development, and energy infrastructure. We're not a foreign owner, not a private-equity holding company, and not a developer parachuting in from out of state.

Will this project be sold to someone else?

Battery storage projects often involve institutional capital partners and operating partners for construction and long-term operations — that's how the energy industry works at this scale. But the commitments made during development, including the community ones, travel with the project. We're not in the business of building a problem and walking away from it.

Why did you pick Jones County?

Because the grid needs storage here, the land works, the people are good, and the Phantom Hill 345 kV substation is one of the most useful interconnection points in ERCOT West. The honest answer is that we looked for the right site for the project, and West Texas was the right answer.

Have a question we didn't answer?

If you're a neighbor, a county or city official, a member of the school board, a member of the press, or just somebody who lives here and wants to know more — we want to hear from you directly. Email us. We answer.
